The Last Potter of Thimi: Keeping Newar Traditions Alive

At 68, Prem Bahadur Prajapati is one of the last master potters in Thimi. His hands, coated in red clay for 50 years, shape vessels using techniques unchanged for 12 generations. But his sons have different plans.

Introduction

In the narrow alleys of Thimi, Bhaktapur, where ancient brick houses lean against each other like old friends, the rhythmic thump-thump-thump of Prem Bahadur Prajapati's pottery wheel echoes through the morning air. At 68, he is one of the last remaining master potters in this historic Newar town.

"My father taught me this craft when I was 12. His father taught him. We go back 12 generations in this courtyard, all making pots. But my sons? They say this is 'old work' for 'old people.'"

Prem Bahadur Prajapati at his traditional pottery wheel in Thimi's historic courtyard

A Master Craftsman's Day

The day begins at 5 AM. Prem Bahadur walks barefoot across the cold courtyard to his workshop‚Äîa small room no larger than a single bed, filled with clay, tools, and memories. The clay comes from the fields of Kavre, red and sticky, perfect for the water vessels and oil lamps that have sustained his family for centuries.

His hands move with practiced ease that defies explanation. A lump of clay becomes a perfect vessel in minutes. The technique‚Äîlearned through decades of repetition‚Äîcannot be written down. "You feel it in your fingers," he explains. "The clay tells you what it wants to become."

Generational Shift

The real story isn't about the pottery‚Äîit's about the people. Prem Bahadur's two sons, both in their thirties, work in IT companies in Kathmandu. They visit on weekends, rolling their eyes at their father's "old-fashioned" work.

"They say there's no money in pottery. That I'm wasting my time. But they don't understand‚Äîthis isn't just work. It's who we are. It's our family's promise to this place."

Across Thimi, the story repeats. Of 47 pottery workshops that existed in 1980, only 8 remain. Most are run by men over 60. The younger generation sees no future in backbreaking work that earns barely Rs. 15,000 ($120) per month.

Update (2024): Since this story was first reported, two more pottery workshops in Thimi have closed. Only 6 remain active.

Market Challenges

The economics are harsh. A water pot that takes Prem Bahadur two hours to make sells for Rs. 200 ($1.60). Plastic containers‚Äîcheaper, lighter, unbreakable‚Äîhave flooded the market. His main customers now are temples who need traditional oil lamps, and a few loyal families who prefer clay for water storage.

"They say my pots are expensive," he says, holding up a perfectly formed vessel. "But this will last 20 years. The plastic breaks in one year and poisons the water."

Uncertain Future

Prem Bahadur has two apprentices‚Äîboth in their forties, both from pottery families, both uncertain about continuing. The government provides no support. Tourism helps marginally, but tourists want cheap souvenirs, not authentic craftsmanship.

Yet he continues. Not for money, but for something deeper. "When I make a pot, I talk to my grandfather. I ask him: am I doing it right? And I hear his voice in the clay."

Reflection

As the sun sets over Thimi, Prem Bahadur cleans his tools and covers his wheel. The courtyard grows quiet. In the distance, the honking of Kathmandu traffic reminds us that the modern world is encroaching.

"I don't know if my grandchildren will do this work. But I know that when I'm gone, some pots I made will still be here. And someone will hold them and wonder about the hands that shaped them. That is enough."

This is the quiet tragedy of Kathmandu Valley‚Äîskills honed over centuries disappearing not with drama, but with the soft click of a door closing, a pottery wheel falling silent, a father's knowledge ending with his generation.
