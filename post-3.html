Kathmandu's Invisible Workforce: Street Vendors' Daily Battle

Every morning at 5 AM, Saraswati rises to sell chatamari near Ratna Park. Her story mirrors thousands of informal workers who keep Kathmandu running while fighting for their right to exist.

Dawn in Kathmandu

The city sleeps, but Saraswati Maharjan, 64, is already awake. By 4:30 AM, she's kneading rice flour dough in her one-room rented space in Gongabu. By 5:30, she's pushing her cart through dark streets toward Ratna Park, where she'll spend the next 12 hours selling chatamari‚Äîtraditional Newari rice crepes.

"I've been doing this for 22 years," she says, her hands moving automatically to spread the dough on her hot plate. "Same spot, same work, every single day except Dashain."

The Invisible Economy

Saraswati is part of Kathmandu's massive informal economy‚Äîunregistered, unprotected, and largely ignored by city planners. She pays no taxes, receives no benefits, and has no legal right to the space she occupies. Yet she provides an essential service: affordable food for the city's working poor.

Her customers are rickshaw drivers, office peons, students, and day laborers. For Rs. 30 ($0.25), they get two hot chatamari‚Äîenough to fuel a morning's work. "If I weren't here," she explains, "these people would go hungry. The restaurants are too expensive."

Daily Harassment

The hardest part of Saraswati's day isn't the physical labor‚Äîit's the constant harassment. Municipal police (nagarik) arrive regularly, demanding bribes or threatening confiscation. Sometimes they take her cart, forcing her to pay Rs. 500-1000 to get it back.

"Yesterday they came at 10 AM. Said I was blocking the footpath. But where else can I go? They took my cart. I had to borrow money to get it back. Today I made no profit."

This extortion is systematic. Vendors estimate they pay 10-15% of their daily income in bribes. It's a tax on the poor, collected by the powerful, with no receipt or recourse.

Resilience

Despite everything, Saraswati persists. She supports two grandchildren through her work. She pays rent, buys food, and saves a little each month. She's part of a vendors' association that advocates for their rights‚Äîthough progress is slow.

"I could stop. Go back to my village. But what would I do there? Here, I have my dignity. I earn my own money. Nobody can tell me I'm worthless."

Fighting for Change

In 2023, street vendors formed the "Kathmandu Valley Informal Workers Union" with 3,200 members. They're demanding designated vending zones, protection from harassment, and access to social security. The city government has promised action, but vendors remain skeptical.

For Saraswati, the fight continues one chatamari at a time. As Kathmandu modernizes with malls and chain restaurants, she and thousands like her represent something older and more essential: the human-scale economy that actually feeds the city.

"They want to make Kathmandu like Singapore. Clean, modern, rich. But Singapore doesn't have room for people like me. Where would we go?"
