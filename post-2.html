Rebuilding Dolakha: Women Leading Earthquake Recovery

After the 2015 earthquake destroyed their homes, women in Dolakha didn't wait for help. They formed collectives, learned masonry, and rebuilt their village stone by stone.

The Day Everything Changed

April 25, 2015, started like any other Saturday in Tukucha village, Dolakha district. By 11:56 AM, everything had changed. The 7.8 magnitude earthquake didn't just shake the ground‚Äîit shattered the entire social fabric of this remote mountain community.

"We were in the fields planting potatoes," remembers Mina Tamang, 45, who lost her home and both her parents that day. "When we ran back, the village was just... gone. Dust everywhere. Nothing standing."

Tukucha village in Dolakha district, three years after the earthquake. Reconstruction was still ongoing when this photo was taken.

Waiting That Never Ended

For weeks after the earthquake, Tukucha's residents waited. Waited for government aid that was promised but slow to arrive. Waited for NGOs that came with assessments but no concrete help. Waited for reconstruction funds that seemed to disappear in Kathmandu bureaucracy.

"Men sat in the relief camp talking about what the government would do. They waited for someone to come save us. But I looked at my two daughters sleeping under a tarp and thought: we cannot wait anymore."

Mina's story was echoed across the village. While men discussed and debated, women were the ones fetching water from broken pipes, cooking in makeshift kitchens, comforting children, and tending to the elderly. They were the ones who saw that waiting was a luxury they couldn't afford.

The Women's Collective

Three weeks after the earthquake, Mina convened a meeting of 15 women in the remains of the village school. They formed the "Tukucha Women's Reconstruction Collective" with a simple, revolutionary idea: they would rebuild themselves.

"We had nothing," Mina recalls. "No money, no materials, no skills. But we had our hands, our determination, and each other."

Update (2024): The Tukucha Women's Reconstruction Collective now has 47 active members and has inspired similar groups in 12 neighboring villages.

Learning to Build

Their first challenge: learning masonry. In Dolakha's patriarchal society, construction was men's work. But necessity has a way of breaking barriers. The women approached Rajendra, a master mason whose own home had collapsed.

"He laughed at first," remembers Sita Lohar, 38. "But when he saw we were serious, he agreed to teach us. For every house we helped him rebuild, he would teach us the skills."

Women from the collective laying stone foundations for a new home in Tukucha village

For three months, the women learned. They learned to cut stone, mix mortar, read levels, and understand structural integrity. They worked from sunrise to sunset, their hands calloused and strong. By the end, they had rebuilt Rajendra's house‚Äîand gained the skills to rebuild their own.

Rebuilding Together

The collective's approach was systematic. They rebuilt homes one by one, prioritizing families with elderly members and young children. Each woman contributed what she could‚Äîlabor, food for the workers, or childcare during construction days.

They also revolutionized building techniques. Traditional stone houses had collapsed because of weak mortar and heavy roofs. The women, listening to engineers who visited the area, incorporated reinforced concrete bands and lighter roofing materials‚Äîadaptations that made homes safer while respecting traditional designs.

"We didn't just rebuild houses. We rebuilt our community. We learned that we are capable‚Äîthat we don't need to wait for men or government to save us."

A New Legacy

Five years after the earthquake, Tukucha stands rebuilt‚Äînot just physically, but socially transformed. The women who learned masonry now earn good wages working across the district. The collective has expanded into a savings group, a business cooperative, and a political force that village elders can no longer ignore.

Mina Tamang, once a subsistence farmer, now leads training programs for women's groups across Dolakha. Her daughters, who watched their mother transform from victim to leader, are studying engineering and architecture.

"When the earthquake came, it took everything from us," Mina reflects. "But it also gave us something: it showed us our own strength. We are not the same women we were before."
