Guardians of Bagmati: The Fishing Families Who Remain

Along the Bagmati River, generations of fishing families preserve their traditions and resilience amidst Kathmandu's rapid urban change.

  2. Tradition and Ritual
  3. Challenges Today
  4. Resilience and Adaptation

Morning on the River

Ramesh casts his net into the Bagmati River with hands trained by decades of practice. His father taught him, and his grandfather before that. The river is not just a livelihood—it is his heritage.

By sunrise, the families along the river are already at work. Nets mended, small boats launched, meals prepared by wives and daughters. Children follow elders, learning the rhythm of water and fish.

Tradition and Ritual

Every household maintains small shrines along the riverbank. Festivals and water blessings punctuate the year, binding families to both faith and nature. Songs, stories, and techniques are passed down orally, preserving culture in a way no school could.

During major festivals, families gather on the banks. Nets are blessed, offerings float downstream, and prayers are said for bountiful catches. These rituals are a reminder that their survival is tied to respect for the river.

"The river is our teacher, our provider, and our home. Without it, we are nothing."

Challenges Today

Pollution, encroachment, and urbanization have drastically reduced fish populations. Plastic debris and untreated sewage flow into the river, making it harder to catch enough fish to sustain livelihoods.

Younger generations are drawn to city jobs. Bikash, Ramesh’s son, worries whether fishing alone will sustain them. "I love the river," he says, "but will it feed my family in the years to come?"

"We are custodians, but we are also at risk. The river is shrinking, and our choices are shrinking with it."

Resilience and Adaptation

Families innovate—some try eco-tourism or sustainable fish farming. NGOs run cleanups and environmental education. Ramesh reflects, "The river has always adapted. We must too."

Women in these communities play an indispensable role. They prepare nets, process fish for market, and manage finances. Their knowledge of seasonal cycles ensures that fishing remains sustainable.

Looking Ahead

Despite challenges, the fishing families continue to pass traditions forward. Workshops, community groups, and local campaigns aim to keep the Bagmati clean and its life alive.

They are quiet custodians of Kathmandu's history, ecology, and culture. Their resilience is a lesson: progress does not require abandoning tradition, and heritage can survive even amidst a city’s rapid change.

"We do not just fish; we preserve a way of life, one net at a time."
