Guardians of Bagmati: The Fishing Families Who Remain

A journey into the lives of the resilient fishing families of Bagmati River, preserving traditions amidst the changing city.

Generations on the Water

Along the winding banks of the Bagmati River, where the city hums just beyond the trees, a quiet way of life persists. Ramesh, a wiry man in his fifties, casts his net with the ease of someone who has done this since childhood. His hands, calloused and steady, speak of decades on the river, while his eyes scan the surface for ripples, currents, and fish movement. “My father taught me the Bagmati,” he says. “And his father before him. The river is our life, our home, our history.”

The Work is Hard, but Meaningful

Sunita, one of the younger members of the community, recalls a day when a heavy monsoon flooded the banks. “I was afraid the nets would wash away,” she says, “but my uncle told me, ‘The Bagmati tests us, but it never abandons us. Be careful, be patient.’ We rescued the nets together and ended up with one of the best catches of the season.”

Living in Harmony with Tradition

The families maintain a rhythm that the city often ignores. They rise before dawn, fish through the morning, and retreat to prepare meals and mend equipment before the afternoon heat. Houses along the river are modest, built to withstand occasional flooding, and often adorned with symbols of protection—small shrines, prayer flags, and offerings left on the banks. Even the youngest children know the chants and rituals by heart, echoing centuries of reverence for the river.

Challenges of Modernization

Pollution from upstream, construction, and waste dumping has changed the river dramatically. Fish populations have dwindled, and some traditional methods are no longer sustainable. Younger generations are tempted by opportunities in the city, abandoning the craft that has sustained their families for generations. “I want to continue fishing,” says Bikash, Ramesh’s son, “but it’s hard to see a future here.”

Anecdotes from the River

One evening, as the sun sank behind the distant hills, nets were stretched across the water, lanterns flickering, casting reflections that danced on the surface. A neighbor called out, “Careful, don’t let the current take the basket!” Laughter followed as a young boy waded through the water to retrieve it. Moments like these reveal the intricate dance of human skill and nature’s unpredictability.

Preserving Cultural Identity

Fishing along the Bagmati is more than an occupation—it is a cultural identity. Songs, chants, and stories accompany the work, often reflecting local history and legends. Festivals like the annual water blessing draw the community together, even attracting outsiders who wish to witness the living traditions.

Lessons from the Guardians

Observing these families, one realizes that the Bagmati is more than a river; it is a teacher. Patience, community, respect for nature, and resilience are all lessons embedded in daily life. These families remind us that progress does not always mean abandoning tradition.

Hope Amidst Change

Local NGOs and environmental groups have started programs to clean stretches of the river and educate residents about sustainable fishing. Some young members are experimenting with fish farming and eco-tourism, blending modern ideas with traditional knowledge. “The river has always adapted,” Ramesh says. “We must, too. But never forget where we come from.”

Conclusion

The fishing families of the Bagmati River are quiet heroes of Kathmandu Valley. Their lives may go unnoticed by most, but they carry forward a legacy of connection, skill, and reverence for nature. Listening to their voices is a reminder that the river belongs not just to the city, but to everyone who has learned to live with it, respect it, and honor it.
